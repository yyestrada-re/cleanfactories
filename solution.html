A GREEN RECOVERY:

In light of the COVID-19 pandemic, we have observed the impact of lockdown on environmental factors — air quality has improved across the globe, industrial dumping has decreased and energy usage has declined. This brief period of environmental headway is largely due to the slowdown of industrial production and as lockdowns begin to ease, it has become increasingly apparent that we capitalize on this brief period of environmental respite and work towards implementing a “green” recovery. But what exactly does that mean? Well, we have a few suggestions:





here's what we can do:

Air

  1. Increase in the use of scrubbers in factories

    • We propose using more scrubbers, which remove pollutants from factories before they leave the smokestacks, to reduce the levels of air pollution.
    • The graph on the right shows the necessity of removing greenhouse gases from the atmosphere in order to slow the rate of global warming.
    • Scrubbers remove 98% of sulfur from emitted gases.

  2. Expand the Clean Air Act

    • We also suggest expanding the Clean Air Act, which gives factories incentives to limit their sulfur dioxide emissions below a certain level. These actions will take steps towards improving our air quality.

Water

  1. Promoting pollution prevention (P2) practices through employee training

    • We propose that at all factories, or at more factories, the employees receive training in terms of having the knowledge they need in order to minimize waste generation. Employees could learn more about spill/leak prevention methods. Ultimately, employees would know more about source reduction. According to the EPA website, “the opportunities for source reduction are not realized as existing regulations focus upon treatment and disposal.”
    • Using less toxic chemicals in factories

  2. Encourage factories to utilize the EPA’s Pollution Prevention resources

  3. Regulating more chemicals found in water

    • While the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has identified and regulates 94 chemicals that are commonly found in contaminated water, there are still many other chemicals that the EPA has not accounted for that could be potentially dangerous to animal, plant and/or human life. We propose that we will reach out to the EPA in hopes of working with them to identify these other potentially-harmful pollutants and begin to regulate them.

Energy

  1. Switching to renewable energies

    • We propose that we use this opportunity to transition to renewable energy and begin shutting down coal power plants. If the industrial industry transitions to full reliance on green energy many other sectors are likely to follow, furthermore carbon emissions would drop a substantial amount, slowing global warming.
    • According to the EPA, switching to renewable energy sources will prevent the emission of greenhouse gases, limit air pollution, and create jobs.

Reaching Out

  1. Writing to local factories and encouraging them to make a change.

    • While there is only so much we can do on an individual level, we have to power to put pressure on local factories and encourage them to make a change. After all, industrial pollution does not have isolated effects. It affects you, your neighbor, and your overall community. So use your voice to incite change!
    • Use the templates provided to urge factories near you to practice more sustainable methods of production.
